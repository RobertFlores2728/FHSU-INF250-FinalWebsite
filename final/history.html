History

The beginnings of Formula One, popularly written as Formula 1 or F1, can be traced back to the racing scenes of post-WWII Europe. It was initially called Formula A, the word 'Formula' refering to construction regulations that were placed on the cars and 'A' (now 'One') signalling the highest category sanctioned by the Federation Internationale de l’Automobile, or FIA. The first championship race was held in 1950 at the Silverstone Circuit in England. Since then, the motorsport has continued to evolve and now has racing circuits in every corner of the world. This page aims to give a light introduction to the history of F1.

The 50s

The 50s were dominated by mostly Italian manufacturers including Alfa Romeo, Ferrari, Maserati, as well as the German manufacturor Mercedes-Benz. One key driver of the era would be Juan Manuel Fangio, from Argentina, who won the championship 5 times with 5 different manufacturers and held records that would last for decades. For instance, the 1957 season was where Fangio won his 4th consecutive drivers’ title, something that would not be broken for nearly 50 years.

The 60s and 70s

The 60s and 70s can be safely described as the era of British dominance, with British manufacturers including Lotus, Brabham, Tyrell, among others, winning 13 out of the 20 championships and led the sport in engineering and racing prowess. Drivers from this era include Jim Clark, Graham Hill, Jackie Stewart, Jack Brabham, Emerson Fittipaldi, and Nikki Lauda.

The 80s and 90s

The 80s and 90s saw the continued dominance by British manufacturers with 16 out of the 20 championships being won by either Williams or Mclaren. Iconic drivers from this era include Nelson Piquet, Alain Prost, Ayrton Senna, Nigel Mansell, Damon Hill, Mika Hakkinen, and Michael Schumacher.

The 2000s

The 2000s saw the end of British dominance, with British teams only having won twice as of 2019 since 2000. Ferrari returned as a dominate manufacturer with the rise of Michael Schumacher to become the driver with the most championships at 7. Aside from Schumacher, drivers Fernando Alonso, Lewis Hamilton, Kimi Raikonnen, and Jenson Button all won championships and are the modern day legends of the sport.

The Current Era

The 2010s are just about wrapped up and have resulted in a decade dominated by Austria's Red Bull and later Germany's Mercedes, with Sebastian Vettel, Lewis Hamilton, and Nico Rosberg winning 4, 4, and 1 championships, respectively, each out of the 9 years. The 2019 season currently has Lewis Hamilton leading with Mercedes, who is on track to win his 6th championship nearing Schumacher's record of 7.
